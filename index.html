Rules are Toxic

The Goal

We want to be productive.

Google's large study investigated teamwork:

re:Work, aka Project Aristotle

One factor dominates in predicting team performance:

Psychological Safety

Psychological Safety

Team members experience psychological safety when they are confident enough to,

  • Make mistakes
  • Ask for help
  • Raise tough issues
  • Accept differences in others
  • Trust coworkers to act in good faith

Other Factors

Psychological safety is more effective than commonly opimized-for factors:

  • Experience Level
  • Hours Worked
  • Strong Team Identity (Monoculture)

Competitive workplaces are the past, Psychological Safety is the future.

Let's Set Some Rules

Programmers are accustomed to solving problems by making rules.

  • Code of Conduct - foster diversity, inhibit bullying
  • Coding Standards - define clean code

These are important documents to have, but do they solve the problem?

Bad Faith Actors

People acting in the best interest of the team don't need rules to tell them right from wrong.

Bullies can be empowered by rules because loop-holes imply areas of permitted (but morally reprehensible) behaviour.

Rules cannot stop people from acting in bad faith.

Scott Tucker

Professional race car driver, pay-day loan empire founder, poster boy for bad faith adherence to rules.

Convicted of racketeering, 16 year prison sentence, $1.3B fine is the largest in FTC history.

His legal defense: he believed that technically he was obeying the law.

Agile by Dictate

Mandatory stand-ups, code reviews, test coverage = productivity?

  • Forced communication becomes about insecurities and signalling rather than surfacing blockers.
  • Token code reviews focus on cosmetic issues.
  • Unit tests can achieve 100% coverage without validation of crucial behaviour.

Alternatives

Rules are often mistaken for,

  • Guidelines - informative, educate
  • Processes - prescriptive, absolve responsibility

Rules are punative. They are not instructions in this context, but rather they assume bad intent and are useful when deciding how to assess penalties after the damage is done.

"More" Illegal

A colleague expressed frustration about composting.

"Failure to compost should be illegal in Vancouver."

"But it is. One could be fined for that."

Exasperated that the problem is occurring regardless,

"Well then it should be more illegal."

More Illegal?

When we want stronger laws, we assess tougher penalties.

Much effort has been expended exploring why this doesn't work.

In a nutshell, rule-breakers don't expect to be caught.

Some laws carry the death penalty (although not in Canada) and are still broken.

Selective Enforcement

Occurs when a rule isn't worth applying consistently.

The moderators / enforcers become bullies by wielding rules against people they don't like.

This is a strong sign that a rule (or system of rules) is invalid.

Cider House Rules

Occurs when the rules don't apply to the rule-makers.

Such rules typically discriminate against a selective group of people.

Consider that a panel comprised of men should not set rules about how women ought to act.

Regulatory Capture

Occurs when pioneers in an industry set rules that inhibit new competitors.

Frequently happens when entrenched parties are the best practices experts.

Consider that a Coding Standard which only applies to new code affects new hires disproportionately.

Fix Your Processes

Rather than punishing mistakes through rules, make it easier for people to do the right thing.

Education helps, set people up for success.

Recognize that some people don't see the harm in hurting others.

Living Documents

Like any documentation, rules are subject to addendums, rewrites, and obsolescense.

Involve human judgement to guard against rules doing more harm than good.

Plan for your rules to go out of date.
